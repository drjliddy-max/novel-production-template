## Prompt for the AI

You are drafting hero or landing page copy for John Liddy. Read these files first, before drafting a single word:

- `style-guide/JOHN_LIDDY_VOICE.md` — voice rules, non-negotiable
- This file — hero copy conventions

Hero copy is the tightest form in John's writing ecosystem. A hero headline gets two seconds of reader attention before they scroll or leave. Every word has to do double duty. Most hero sections should be five sentences or fewer total across the headline, subhead, and primary CTA.

### Page specifics to fill in

- **Site:** [theparticipationeffect.com / adaauditreport.com / daily-rise.com / other]
- **Page:** [home / About / specific landing page]
- **Reader on arrival:** [who is on this page, what they were searching for, what they expect]
- **Goal of the section:** [the one action you want from them, e.g. "buy the book" / "request an audit" / "open the practice"]
- **Headline length cap:** [usually 8-12 words]
- **Subhead length cap:** [usually 15-25 words]
- **Primary CTA text:** [the button label, 2-5 words]
- **Trust elements available:** [reviews, ratings, before/after screenshots, social proof, etc.]

### Hero structure

**Headline.** One sentence. Names the reader's actual problem or the actual outcome they want. No clever wordplay that sacrifices clarity. No abstract benefit language. The reader should know within two seconds what this site is about and whether it applies to them.

**Subhead.** One or two sentences. Adds the specific how, the differentiator, or the proof. This is where you earn the click on the CTA.

**Primary CTA.** Two to five words. Plain verb plus object. "Buy the book." "Run my audit." "Open the practice." Not "Click here." Not "Learn more." Specific.

**Optional secondary line.** A risk-reduction sentence ("Free chapter, no signup required") or a proof line ("Used by 500+ small businesses"). Only include if it earns its place.

**Optional trust strip.** Star rating, review snippet, screenshot, or social proof. Only include if real and specific.

### Voice rules

The standard voice rules from `style-guide/JOHN_LIDDY_VOICE.md` all apply. The ones that matter most for hero copy:

- Open with the idea, no throat-clearing
- Specific vocabulary, never abstract ("clenched jaw" not "stress")
- No em dashes
- No "However," "Furthermore"
- Negate before revealing if it earns its keep: "Not a typical self-help book. Tools that work."
- Every word carries weight or gets cut

### Patterns that work for John specifically

These are hero patterns that fit his voice:

**Problem-statement headline + practical-promise subhead:**
- Headline: "Stop running on empty. Start seeing more."
- Subhead: "A practical framework for thinking clearly when life feels uncertain. Two-minute daily practice. No woo."

**Negation-revelation headline + specific-promise subhead:**
- Headline: "Not therapy. Not manifestation. Tools that work."
- Subhead: "The Participation Effect gives you the daily practice that comes out of two decades of clinical experience and one survived breakdown."

**Direct-question headline + answer subhead:**
- Headline: "Is your website ADA compliant?"
- Subhead: "Run the free scan. See exactly what to fix. Get a clean report you can send to your developer in five minutes."

### Patterns to avoid

- Abstract benefit language: "Transform your life," "Unlock your potential"
- Clever wordplay that sacrifices clarity: "Re-rise to the occasion"
- Industry jargon: "Synergy," "Holistic wellness journey," "Optimization"
- Hedge words: "Could," "May," "Might help you"
- Fake urgency: "Limited time," "Spots filling fast"
- Stock self-help phrasing: "Live your best life," "Find your why"

### Self-check before returning the draft

1. Does the headline name a real reader problem or a real outcome they want?
2. Is the headline under 12 words?
3. Does the subhead add specific information beyond the headline?
4. Does the CTA verb match the actual action the user will take?
5. Is there any abstract language that should be replaced with concrete language?
6. Are there any em dashes or banned transitions?
7. Read it out loud. Does it sound like John talking, or like a marketing committee?

If any fail, rewrite before returning.
